## Problem

People arrived at salary pages during high-stakes moments - a live offer, an appraisal, a career move - needing ammunition for a negotiation. Instead they got a flat average for a broad role like "Engineer": too generic to explain take-home pay, fixed-versus-variable structure, or whether someone with their exact experience was being lowballed.

## Approach

We reframed the product from a salary database into a negotiation engine. The page moved away from one broad gross number and toward the levers people actually use in salary conversations: monthly take-home, pay structure, specific-role benchmarks, and experience-scaled comparisons. Just as importantly, unreliable data was suppressed instead of dressed up as insight.

## 3 Decisions

1. **Data integrity over feature density** - if the salary curve was statistically noisy, the widget hid itself because a number you cannot defend is worse than no number.
2. **Monthly liquidity over gross CTC** - the page prioritized take-home pay and fixed-versus-variable structure because people negotiate and budget in real monthly cash.
3. **Specificity before depth** - broad role pages acted as a funnel into department-specific views, so users did not make decisions from misleading blended averages.
